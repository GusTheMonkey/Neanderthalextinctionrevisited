Why Did Neanderthals Go Extinct?

Earlier hypotheses posited that Neanderthals were simply outcompeted by anatomically modern humans; however, contemporary research increasingly attributes their extinction to intrinsic factors such as genetic vulnerabilities and susceptibility to disease (Degioanni et al., 2019; Vaesen et al., 2019).

Genetic Weaknesses

One critical factor contributing to Neanderthal extinction was the demographic fragility of their populations. Characterized by low genetic diversity and high levels of inbreeding, these small, isolated groups experienced pronounced genetic drift and homozygosity (Green et al., 2010; Vaesen et al., 2019). Genetic analyses reveal the presence of deleterious mutations in immune-related genes, such as UNC13D and MOGS, impairing their ability to combat pathogens effectively (Toncheva et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022).

Neanderthal genomes exhibit pathogenic variants that likely contributed to immunological vulnerabilities (Green et al., 2010; Zhou et al., 2022).

The Allee Effect

The Allee effect—a phenomenon where small populations suffer from reduced fitness due to difficulties in mate finding and maintaining genetic variability—likely exacerbated the Neanderthals’ decline (Vaesen et al., 2019). Modeling studies suggest that even minor fluctuations in fertility rates or survival probabilities would have made demographic recovery virtually impossible (Degioanni et al., 2019; Vaesen et al., 2019). Compounding this, evidence indicates that younger Neanderthal females may have experienced decreased fertility, further diminishing the viability of their populations over time.

Simulation models illustrate the severe demographic pressures facing Neanderthal populations (Vaesen et al., 2019).

Vulnerability to Disease

In addition to their genetic impairments, Neanderthals' low genetic diversity rendered them highly vulnerable to infectious diseases. As emerging pathogens spread—potentially introduced through contact with Homo sapiens—their limited immunogenetic repertoire left them disproportionately susceptible to epidemics (Zhou et al., 2022; Toncheva et al., 2023). These repeated biological shocks likely precipitated local collapses, contributing to their ultimate extinction across Eurasia (Finlayson & Carrión, 2007).

Neanderthal immune systems were insufficiently equipped to handle novel pathogens, a factor intensified by low genetic variability (Zhou et al., 2022).
