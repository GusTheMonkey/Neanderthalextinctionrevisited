Why Did Neanderthals Go Extinct?

While many once believed that Neanderthals were outcompeted by early humans, recent studies suggest their extinction was more likely due to genetic vulnerabilities and disease.

Genetic Weaknesses

One major factor was their small, isolated populations, making them highly susceptible to inbreeding and genetic homogeneity.

Research shows that Neanderthals carried harmful mutations like UNC13D and MOGS, linked to severe immune deficiencies—leaving them vulnerable to infections.

Neanderthal genomes show harmful mutations that contributed to their extinction.

The Allee Effect

The Allee effect—where small populations struggle with survival—further weakened Neanderthals. Difficulty finding mates and declining fertility rates amplified their decline before Homo sapiens' arrival.

Vaesen, K. simulates how Allee effects and other demographic variables impacted Neanderthal survival.

Vulnerability to Disease

Low genetic diversity also meant they were defenseless against new diseases. Zhou et al. (2022) suggest that even minor infections could have devastated entire communities.
